# From Critique to Construction: Priorities for Signed-Language Writing Infrastructure

**A constructive synthesis for research, tooling, standards, and adoption**

The review series has a diagnostic purpose, but diagnosis is not the end of the work.

If the series is right about the field's recurring problems, then the next task is not to repeat them more sharply. It is to identify what should actually be built, studied, stabilized, and compared next.

One recurring problem is later over-reading: a source solves one layer, and later discussion treats that result as if it solved the whole signed-text path. Construction begins by preserving the solved layer while naming the layers that still need work.

This paper therefore shifts the emphasis from critique to construction. It treats SignWriting and Formal SignWriting as the strongest currently documented path to durable signed-language text infrastructure, while also treating that position as the beginning of a serious program rather than its conclusion.

For the broader theory-facing bridge behind this constructive agenda, see *Signed Writing as a Pressure Test for General Writing Theory*, the companion paper attached to the Meletis and Dürscheid review.

## The point of construction

The field no longer benefits much from cycling through the weakest old disputes:

- whether signed languages can be written at all
- whether video replaces writing
- whether all signed writing must resemble alphabetic writing
- whether spatial composition disqualifies a system from counting as writing

Those arguments still appear, but they no longer mark the leading edge of the problem.

The more serious questions are constructive:

- what text infrastructure already exists
- what remains incomplete
- what evidence is still needed
- what authority, data-governance, and stewardship boundaries must be named
- what comparisons would actually move the field forward
- and how signed writing can be made easier to use, study, preserve, and extend

If critique has value, it is because it makes these next questions clearer.

## What the review series has clarified

Taken together, the review series supports five working conclusions.

### 1. Signed writing must be judged as writing, not only as notation

The literature repeatedly collapses:

- notation
- public writing
- semantic representation
- encoding
- rendering

The first constructive requirement is to keep those layers distinct so that different projects can be compared fairly.

### 2. Durable text is the real infrastructure threshold

The most important difference between approaches is not visual style.

It is whether they provide a durable path to:

- authored written form
- preservable text
- searchable text
- processable text
- reproducible text across tools and time

This is the threshold at which the field moves from interesting representation to real writing infrastructure.

### 3. SignWriting currently provides the strongest documented full path

The review series does not need to retreat from that conclusion.

SignWriting plus Formal SignWriting currently appears to provide the broadest documented end-to-end path across:

- public writing
- structured written signs
- text modeling
- editors and fonts
- dictionaries and corpora
- software workflows
- and real institutional use

The claim is comparative completeness at present.

### 4. Alternative systems still matter

A constructive field does not flatten alternatives into irrelevance.

Adjacent-purpose systems may remain valuable for:

- transcriptional precision
- pedagogical bridge use
- handwriting experimentation
- semantic formalization
- community identity
- local or niche communicative practice

The constructive task is not to deny those roles, but to describe them clearly and compare them at the correct level.

That means being more explicit than the review series has always been so far. For example:

- `HamNoSys` remains valuable as a linguistic transcription and documentation tool
- `Stokoe-style systems` remain historically and analytically important as research notations
- `ASL-phabet` can still be discussed as a pedagogical bridge in alphabetically oriented literacy settings
- `ASLwrite` and related community-oriented spatial experiments keep alive questions of community style, handwriting practice, and visually compact everyday inscription
- `AZee` and related work remain important where the main target is semantic formalization, synthesis, and human-editable structured representation

None of those roles is trivial.

The point of comparison is not to erase them. It is to ask whether any of them currently provide the same full path to durable public text infrastructure.

### 5. The field's open questions are empirical and infrastructural

The weakest remaining discussions are often still conceptual.

The strongest remaining questions are more practical:

- reading and readability
- handwriting fluency
- adoption trajectories
- interoperability
- educational outcomes
- corpus growth
- software maintenance
- standards compatibility

Those are the places where real progress has to happen.

## Priority 1: Evidence beyond plausibility

The field needs more than arguments that signed writing is possible, desirable, or conceptually coherent.

It needs stronger evidence on use, uptake, and consequence.

The highest-value evidence priorities include:

- independent longitudinal literacy studies
- reading studies using actual signed text rather than gloss surrogates
- handwriting and composition studies
- adoption mapping across institutions and regions
- comparative work against video-plus-gloss and bilingual baselines

The next step is to move from feasibility and existence arguments toward evidence about performance, uptake, and educational effect.

## Priority 2: Corpora and reusable signed text

A field cannot mature on isolated exemplars alone.

It needs reusable text.

That means:

- expanding text corpora
- improving corpus discoverability
- preserving earlier materials
- supporting text reuse across dictionaries, publications, and research
- making corpora suitable for both human reading and computational analysis
- keeping written signs, lexical records, media records, corpus records, dataset items, and rights metadata distinct

This is where SignWriting's current strengths matter most. A real text model and a real writing practice make corpus work possible in a way that video-only ecosystems do not.

But corpus growth does not happen automatically. It depends on:

- tools
- curation
- metadata
- rights and consent practices
- maintenance
- and institutional continuity

## Priority 3: Tooling that lowers the cost of writing

A real writing path is not only a theoretical model. It is an authoring environment.

The next generation of tooling should make signed writing:

- faster to compose
- easier to revise
- easier to publish
- easier to search
- easier to exchange

That includes work on:

- editors
- input methods
- fonts and rendering
- validation tools
- conversion utilities
- corpus interfaces
- and publishing pipelines

The field should not confuse the existence of tools with the maturity of tools. The current base is real. The next task is reducing friction.

## Priority 4: Interoperability and migration

A durable ecosystem requires more than internal coherence.

It requires pathways between:

- old and new datasets
- FSW and SWU workflows
- Unicode-facing and production-facing systems
- web publication and archival storage
- research tools and community tools

This is one reason the standards question remains live. The challenge is not just whether characters have names. It is whether signed text can move across systems without losing writer-selected structure or becoming harder to use.

The constructive program here includes:

- explicit migration pathways
- format comparison
- preservation strategy
- and compatibility work where possible

## Priority 5: Education without alphabetic defaultism

Educational use remains one of the field's strongest motivations and one of its thinnest evidence bases.

A constructive agenda should avoid two mistakes:

- treating English transfer as the only meaningful educational measure
- or pretending bilingual realities do not matter

What is needed instead is a fuller educational research program around:

- signed-language-first writing instruction
- bilingual sequencing
- transfer questions
- teacher preparation
- classroom materials
- and the relation between everyday writing fluency and formal literacy outcomes

This is also where alternative systems may still have narrower contributions. Some may prove useful as bridges, supplements, or local pedagogical tools even if they do not offer a full text infrastructure.

## Priority 6: Handwriting, fluency, and ordinary use

One of the easiest ways to overstate the field's maturity is to focus only on formal structure and software.

Writing systems also live or die by ordinary use.

That means the field needs better understanding of:

- handwriting practice
- composition speed
- notation-to-writing transitions
- everyday messaging and drafting
- reader familiarity
- and community uptake outside specialized circles

This is an area where critics often see a real weakness, and the constructive response is not dismissal. It is sharper study, better tool support, and clearer documentation of where fluency already exists and where it does not.

## Priority 7: Standards engagement without standards reduction

Standards work remains necessary, but the review series has already shown that standardization cannot be reduced to character naming.

The constructive standards agenda should therefore remain tied to:

- written-sign structure
- symbol identity
- migration compatibility
- and production use

This means engaging standards bodies where useful while refusing to let the field's real writing problems be redefined into narrower character problems.

## Priority 8: Broader collaboration without false equivalence

If the field is to grow, it needs broader collaboration:

- with linguists
- literacy researchers
- grapholinguists
- software developers
- corpus builders
- educators
- and standards participants

But collaboration works best when comparative asymmetries remain visible.

A stronger collaborative posture is:

- candid about comparative asymmetries
- generous about niche strengths
- open about unresolved questions
- and explicit about where joint work would be most useful

It should also make room for mixed programs. A field may reasonably use:

- SignWriting for durable authored text
- HamNoSys or related notations for fine-grained linguistic analysis
- AZee-style work for semantic and synthesis pipelines
- and local community experiments to study handwriting, fluency, and adoption

That is not incoherence. It is division of labor.

In practice, that division of labor would mean building workflows where authored SignWriting text can link to analytic notation, semantic representations, video evidence, dictionary records, and datasets without pretending those objects are interchangeable.

That stance earns more trust than either triumphalism or false balance.

## What construction should sound like

The field does not need a softer substitute for critique.

It needs critique that points somewhere.

That means replacing the weakest recurring pattern:

- this approach is incomplete

with a stronger constructive question:

- what exactly would have to be built, shown, tested, or stabilized to move this area forward?

That change in emphasis matters. It turns the review series into a filter for future work rather than a resting place for correct diagnosis.

## A constructive program for the next phase

A serious next-phase program would tie together:

- research on reading, literacy, and adoption
- corpus expansion and preservation
- lower-friction writing tools
- interoperability work
- standards engagement
- data governance and stewardship
- handwriting and ordinary-use study
- and clearer comparative work on alternative systems by function

That program does not require abandoning the central conclusion of the series.

It requires treating that conclusion as a platform for building rather than as a closing argument.

## Final assessment

The most useful outcome of the review series is not that it identifies recurring errors.

It is that it clarifies what the field most needs next.

The strongest constructive conclusion available at present is:

> signed-language writing needs more evidence, more corpus work, better tools, stronger interoperability, and broader collaboration built on real written text rather than on repeated returns to the question of whether writing is possible at all

If critique continues, it should continue in that spirit.

The point is not only to defend a baseline.

The point is to make the next phase of the field easier to build.
