# A Review of Grushkin (2017)

**Writing Signed Languages: What For? What Form? and the question of signed-language writing purpose**

Reviewed source:

- Donald A. Grushkin, "Writing Signed Languages: What For? What Form?"
- *American Annals of the Deaf*, 161(5), 509-527
- Year: 2017
- DOI: `10.1353/aad.2017.0001`

Donald A. Grushkin's 2017 article asks an important question:

> what is signed writing for, and what form should it take?

The article should be read in its educational and practitioner-facing context. It is trying to answer institutional questions about Deaf education, ASL-English biliteracy, signed-language preservation, and classroom practice, not only to produce a detached writing-systems typology.

Its lasting value comes from taking the need for signed writing seriously. Grushkin does not argue that signed languages merely could be written in some abstract sense. He argues that they should be written, that common objections to signed writing are weak, and that writing would materially benefit signing communities in pedagogy, preservation, communication, and literacy.

That is the article's central contribution.

The article has two linked but separable arguments. The first is a broad argument that signed languages should have written forms. The second is a narrower argument that alphabetically based signed writing is preferable in alphabetic-language educational contexts.

Its weakness appears when it moves from the general need for signed writing to a specific orthographic recommendation. Grushkin's alphabetic recommendation is best understood as a biliteracy-transfer hypothesis: alphabetic signed writing may support transfer into English print literacy in alphabetic educational contexts. Read that way, it is substantive and worth investigating.

The problem is that the article does not keep the claim at that level. It treats alphabetic alignment as though it settled the form of signed-language writing more generally. That move is not supported as strongly as the preceding necessity-of-writing argument.

So the article is strongest on why signed languages need writing, substantively interesting but overclaimed on alphabetic transfer, and weak as a general orthographic prescription.

Framework judgment:

| Field | Judgment |
| --- | --- |
| Layer-level judgment | Correct and useful as a necessity-of-writing argument for signed languages, and substantive as a biliteracy-transfer hypothesis. |
| Full-path judgment | Not full-path adequate as a general orthographic prescription, because the alphabetic recommendation does not account sufficiently for signed written units, spatial organization, text modeling, infrastructure, or evidence that alphabetic transfer should govern signed-language writing. |
| Main layer risk | Overextending a plausible educational-transfer claim into a general writing-system conclusion. |

Refinement:

`Strong on the Need for Signed Writing; Overclaimed on Alphabetic Transfer; Weak as General Orthographic Prescription`

## What the article sets out to do

Grushkin frames the article around familiar objections to written signed language and a set of hoped-for benefits. He begins by noting that signing communities are often treated, and sometimes treat themselves, as fundamentally "oral" communities without writing. He then argues that this should not be accepted as natural or final.

His project has two parts:

- to advocate the development and dissemination of a written form of signed languages
- to argue in favor of an alphabetically based system over other possible orthographic formats

The second goal matters because it shapes the whole article. This is not only an argument for writing as such. It is an argument for a particular kind of written signed language.

That context makes the English-biliteracy concern understandable. In ASL-English educational settings, an alphabetically organized signed-writing system might be proposed as a bridge into English print literacy. That is a real educational hypothesis.

It does not, by itself, settle signed-language orthography.

The article then moves through four main stages:

1. objections to written signed languages
2. benefits of developing and accepting them
3. literacy and biliteracy considerations
4. comparative reasoning about orthographic form

This overall structure is coherent. The first half builds a broad pro-writing case. The second half narrows too quickly from transfer considerations to an alphabetic recommendation.

## What Grushkin establishes well

### 1. It argues convincingly that signed languages benefit from writing

This is the article's strongest and most durable contribution.

Grushkin argues that signed languages benefit from writing for:

- literacy development
- pedagogy
- preservation
- internal community communication
- discussion of signed language in signed language
- the creation and storage of literature, history, and cultural record

These arguments are not trivial. They directly answer a persistent background assumption in signed-language discourse: that video is enough, or that signed languages do not need writing in the same way spoken languages do.

Grushkin is right to reject that assumption.

He understands that writing is not merely a way to archive language mechanically. It is a social and cognitive technology. It supports:

- skimming
- scanning
- searching
- easy rereading
- routine text production
- communication across time and distance

Those are writing-specific affordances. Video can preserve signed performance more exactly, but it does not simply replace writing as a practical textual medium.

That point remains one of the strongest things in the article.

### 2. It is right that gloss is inadequate

Grushkin's critique of gloss is strong and should be preserved.

He points out that glossing:

- depends on another language
- encourages false one-to-one mappings between sign and spoken-language word
- obscures grammatical differences between signed and spoken languages
- makes signed languages appear incomplete or ungrammatical
- keeps signed language trapped inside the written form of the majority language

This is exactly right.

Gloss is not a writing system. It is a research and instructional convenience. It may be useful in narrow contexts, but it does not provide a native written pathway for signed languages.

Grushkin is also right to say that glossing reinforces misconceptions. It can make ASL look like reduced or broken English when what is really being seen is an English-based representation of a structurally different language.

That critique remains fully usable.

### 3. It is right that video is not a substitute for writing

Grushkin correctly distinguishes between recording a performance and having writable text.

Video is valuable. It may indeed preserve more of an actual signed utterance than writing can. But that is not the only criterion that matters. Writing supports ordinary textual practices that video does not support nearly as well:

- quick browsing
- selective rereading
- easy quotation
- direct comparison
- annotation
- searching within documents
- economical routine exchange

This argument is especially important because "use video instead" is one of the most common moves used to dismiss signed writing. Grushkin sees clearly that video and writing are not interchangeable media.

### 4. It sees real community and educational reasons for wanting signed writing

The article is not limited to abstract writing-system theory. It also identifies community uses for written signed language that are easy to underestimate:

- discussing signs directly without resorting to gloss or long prose descriptions
- writing in the community's preferred language
- preserving Deaf literature and history
- supporting explicit ASL instruction
- creating a more stable basis for Deaf bilingual education

Even where one may disagree with the article's later orthographic recommendation, these motivations are real. Grushkin is not solving a fake problem.

### 5. It correctly distinguishes notation from writing

This is another good feature of the article.

Grushkin distinguishes systems meant for linguistic transcription from systems meant for ordinary use. He notes that notation attempts to capture fine detail for analysis, whereas writing needs only enough structure for ordinary comprehension and routine manipulation.

That distinction matters because it blocks a common confusion in the literature. The question is not merely whether sign languages can be transcribed. Of course they can. The question is what kind of writing system is suitable for everyday textual use.

Grushkin asks that question directly, and that makes the article worth reviewing seriously.

## The alphabetic recommendation as transfer hypothesis

The fairest reading of Grushkin's alphabetic recommendation is not that alphabetic signed writing is automatically correct because alphabetic writing is familiar.

In this review, `alphabetic` does not mean English gloss or ordinary English spelling. It refers to a parameter-based signed-language script organized by alphabetic principles and proposed as a bridge for ASL-English biliteracy.

The stronger reading is that alphabetic signed writing may offer a pedagogical bridge in ASL-English biliteracy contexts.

The strongest version of Grushkin's alphabetic argument is not mere resemblance to English print. It is the hypothesis that a parameter-based signed script could support metalinguistic or phonological awareness in ASL and then transfer into English literacy.

That hypothesis has real reasons behind it:

- English print literacy is a major educational demand for many Deaf students in the United States.
- Transfer across languages and writing systems is a legitimate literacy-research question.
- Orthographic familiarity may affect teaching, learning, and institutional uptake.
- A system that resembles alphabetic print may appear easier to integrate into existing schools, materials, and teacher training.

Those considerations should not be dismissed.

But they also should not be converted into an orthographic conclusion without further evidence. A transfer hypothesis is not the same as a general answer to signed-language writing. A classroom bridge claim is not the same as a full-path writing-system claim.

The decisive question is not whether alphabetic similarity has pedagogical value. It may.

The decisive question is whether that similarity produces stronger outcomes than a robust signed-language literacy pathway whose organization differs from English print.

## Where the argument becomes overclaimed

The article's limit appears when the alphabetic transfer hypothesis becomes the governing criterion for signed-language orthography.

### 1. SignWriting and related systems are treated too shallowly as "iconographic"

Grushkin groups SignWriting, ASLwrite, and related visually organized systems under the label "iconographic." He uses this category to oppose them to alphabetic systems and to argue that alphabetic signed writing is better suited for biliteracy with English.

Grushkin grants that iconographic systems can work as writing systems. His objection is not that they are impossible or useless, but that they may be less optimal where English biliteracy is the goal.

This is too shallow as a description of SignWriting.

It is also too shallow as a description of the wider group. Even granting Grushkin's narrower point, the comparison still treats the alphabetic/iconographic contrast as sufficient for judging educational adequacy. That leaves the internal structure of plane-based signed writing underanalyzed. Some community-oriented spatial systems have been real attempts to keep signed writing compact, writable, and stylistically alive in ways that cannot be dismissed as mere pictoriality.

The problem is not that SignWriting contains iconic elements. It does. The problem is that the article treats "iconographic" as though it were a sufficiently explanatory classification by itself. That flattens several important distinctions:

- iconicity versus lack of structure
- plane-based spatial writing versus pictorial approximation
- a stable alphabet of written units versus free-form drawing
- authored spatial relation as written form versus ornamental layout

In other words, the article tends to treat SignWriting as though its visuality were its primary analytical fact. That is not enough.

A serious account of SignWriting has to reckon with it as a structured writing system with a stable inventory, regular composition, and authored spatial relations. By reducing the comparison to alphabetic versus iconographic, the article makes the competing systems easier to dismiss than they should be.

### 2. Alphabetic alignment becomes a standard before it becomes evidence

Grushkin repeatedly evaluates signed writing by asking how readily it aligns with English-style orthographic expectations and English biliteracy strategies. This makes resemblance to alphabetic writing appear as a major virtue before the comparison has been fully argued.

That is not a neutral comparison.

It is a case of `Alphabetic Bias`.

The article should have kept two questions distinct:

- what kind of writing best fits signed language on its own terms?
- what kind of signed writing best supports transfer into English print literacy?

Both questions are legitimate.

They are not identical.

The second may be practically important in many classrooms, but it cannot simply replace the first. Once it does, the inquiry shifts from evaluating signed-language writing as writing to evaluating it by compatibility with a surrounding spoken-language norm.

### 3. The biliteracy argument does not justify the orthographic recommendation as strongly as claimed

Grushkin devotes substantial effort to literacy and biliteracy research. He reviews work on:

- phonological awareness
- deep and shallow orthography
- cross-language transfer
- alphabetic and logographic reading strategies
- interference between writing systems

This gives the article an appearance of strong comparative grounding.

But the actual conclusion drawn from this literature is stronger than the evidence warrants.

The article establishes a plausible claim:

- orthographic form can affect literacy transfer

It also establishes another plausible claim:

- if English biliteracy is a major educational goal, orthographic compatibility may matter

What it does not establish strongly enough is:

- therefore alphabetic signed writing is preferable to SignWriting or other plane-based systems as signed-language writing

That stronger recommendation depends on a chain of assumptions that the article does not sufficiently secure:

- that signed writing should be optimized primarily for transfer into English
- that alphabetic signed writing would in fact provide the best such transfer
- that visually organized or spatial systems would create transfer burdens large enough to outweigh their writing-internal adequacy
- that the educational and linguistic benefits of congruence with English outweigh other representational considerations
- that an alphabetic signed-language system can become strong signed-language literacy in its own right

Those are not trivial assumptions. The article moves across them too quickly.

### 4. The source-literacy problem remains unresolved

The alphabetic recommendation depends on an under-argued assumption:

> an alphabetic approach for signed-language writing can be created and can work well as signed-language writing.

If that assumption were satisfied, the transfer argument would become more serious.

If alphabetic signed writing could support robust signed-language literacy while also creating useful bridges to English print, then it would deserve careful empirical comparison.

But similarity to English print is not enough by itself.

A bridge to English print literacy is only as strong as the signed-language literacy it asks learners to build from. If the signed writing system is structurally weak, hard to read, or poorly fitted to signed language, then alphabetic similarity may create the appearance of transfer while weakening the source literacy.

The comparison should therefore be framed as:

- strong first-language signed-language literacy that differs from English print
- alphabetic signed-language bridge literacy that resembles English print
- English print literacy without a native signed-language writing pathway
- mixed instructional paths that use different systems for different purposes

The article does not give enough evidence to settle among those pathways.

### 5. The written sign is underanalyzed as a structured unit

Grushkin discusses writing-system type, literacy strategy, and orthographic transfer, but he does not sufficiently analyze the written sign as a unit of structure in systems such as SignWriting. The comparison is not really between "pictures" and "letters." It is between different models of what the primary written unit is and how written form is organized.

Without a stronger structural account of the written sign, the article's comparative framework remains too coarse. It can say that one system looks more global and another more alphabetic, but it cannot adequately explain what the spatial system is actually doing as writing.

That leaves the comparison underdescribed at exactly the point where it needs to be strongest.

## The article's mixed treatment of education

This is one of the article's more useful tensions.

Grushkin is very good at identifying educational problems:

- English-centered pedagogy
- overreliance on gloss
- the difficulty of forcing Deaf students to build literacy only through the majority written language
- the value of capitalizing on language strengths rather than deficits

These are important insights.

Grushkin's standardization argument is one of the article's more powerful educational claims, but it also raises questions of community authority and variation that the article does not fully separate from orthographic design.

But once the article reaches orthographic prescription, the educational argument becomes too one-directional. The possibility that a well-designed signed writing system might support literacy precisely because it is structurally adequate to signed language is not given equal weight with the goal of transfer into English.

The same asymmetry also makes it harder for the article to acknowledge that different systems may serve different educational roles. A bridge script optimized for transfer, a community handwriting practice, and a full text infrastructure do not have to be the same thing.

That asymmetry matters. It means the article treats signed writing partly as an educational bridge technology rather than fully as a native writing system in its own right.

Again, that does not ruin the whole article.

It does limit the force of the recommendation.

## What the article actually establishes

The article establishes several important things, and these should be stated clearly.

Its strongest responsible conclusions are:

- signed languages benefit from writing
- common objections to written signed language are weak or overstated
- gloss is not an adequate long-term written solution
- video does not replace writing as routine text technology
- signed writing could support literacy, pedagogy, communication, preservation, and internal discourse
- alphabetic signed writing may be worth investigating as a biliteracy-transfer or bridge hypothesis

These are substantial contributions.

The article does not establish the stronger claims that:

- alphabetic signed writing should be preferred to SignWriting as a general orthographic conclusion
- the "iconographic" label is enough to evaluate the structural adequacy of signed writing systems
- compatibility with English literacy should govern the design of signed writing
- visually organized signed writing systems are best understood through analogy with logography alone
- alphabetic similarity produces stronger literacy outcomes than a structurally adequate signed-language writing pathway

That difference matters.

The article's pro-writing argument is strong.

Its alphabetic transfer hypothesis is worth taking seriously.

Its general orthographic conclusion outruns the evidence offered.

## Review through the critique framework

### Layer identification

The article moves among:

- writing-system advocacy
- literacy and biliteracy theory
- comparative orthographic evaluation
- educational prescription

Those are not identical layers, and the article does not always keep them distinct. It tends to move from educational goals to writing-system judgment too quickly.

Declared dependencies help separate the claims. The necessity-of-writing argument solves a real advocacy and conceptual layer: it shows why signed languages need durable writing rather than gloss or video alone. The alphabetic-transfer hypothesis belongs to the literacy and pedagogy layers. The alphabetic orthographic recommendation, however, assumes layers that are not supplied: a primary written unit adequate to signed language, a text model, conventions, infrastructure, and evidence that alphabetic compatibility should govern the design.

Those layers are not merely deferred. They are needed for the recommendation to carry the weight the article gives it.

### Representation model

The article evaluates signed writing under a spoken-language orthographic expectation, especially an alphabetic one. That biases the comparison before the systems have been described at the appropriate structural level.

### Structural unit analysis

Weak.

The article does not adequately theorize the written sign as a structured unit in plane-based systems. As a result, its treatment of SignWriting remains too shallow to support the strength of its critique.

### Spatial vs linear organization

Mixed.

The article recognizes that signed languages are visual and spatial, but in comparative judgment it tends to treat more linear, horizontally arranged, alphabetic organization as a practical advantage. It does not seriously explore the possibility that spatially organized writing may be more adequate to the language even if less congruent with alphabetic habit.

### Text model and infrastructure

Limited.

The article acknowledges several actual signed writing systems, but gives too little weight to SignWriting as a mature, structured, already-used system with real writing practice and infrastructure. That weakens the comparative argument.

### Claims vs evidence

This is the decisive issue.

The necessity-of-writing argument is stronger than the alphabetic recommendation. The transfer hypothesis is plausible, but the general prescription is not adequately established. The article's strongest pages justify writing. Its less-supported pages prescribe a type of writing more confidently than the evidence allows.

## Main critique categories

- `Alphabetic Bias`
- `Incomplete Infrastructure Engagement`
- `Evidence Overextension`
- `Full-Path Overextension`

## Final assessment

Grushkin's article is important because it presses a real question that much of the literature avoids:

- not whether signed languages can be written in principle
- but why signed languages should be written and what that writing is for

Its best contribution is clear:

- signed languages need writing, and the standard objections to written signed language are weak

That argument remains valuable and deserves to be preserved.

Its alphabetic recommendation is not empty or foolish. It is best read as a biliteracy-transfer hypothesis in ASL-English educational contexts. At that layer, it deserves empirical investigation.

Its weakness is that it treats that hypothesis too much like a settled orthographic truth. Alphabetic alignment is treated as if it settles the form of signed-language writing, rather than as a hypothesis requiring full-path evidence.

That is why the article is useful but not decisive.

It identifies the right problem.

It offers a plausible educational bridge argument.

It does not solve the broader writing-system question well enough.

So the right verdict is neither dismissal nor acceptance without correction.

The article should be read as:

- a strong argument for the necessity of signed writing
- a substantive but under-evidenced argument for alphabetic transfer
- a weak argument for alphabetic signed writing as a general orthographic prescription
- and a clear example of how genuine educational insight can become overextended when transfer into majority-language print becomes the governing standard for signed-language writing
