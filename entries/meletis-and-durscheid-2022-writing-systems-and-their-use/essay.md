# A Review of Meletis and Dürscheid (2022)

**Writing Systems and Their Use and the typological limit of signed language writing**

Reviewed source:

- Dimitrios Meletis and Christa Dürscheid, *Writing Systems and Their Use: An Overview of Grapholinguistics*
- Publisher: De Gruyter
- Year: 2022
- DOI: `10.1515/9783110757835`

Dimitrios Meletis and Christa Dürscheid's *Writing Systems and Their Use* is one of the stronger recent comparative accounts of writing.

A strong book is a better test of a field than a weak one. If signed writing still strains the categories here, the pressure probably is not local to one source.

The question in this review is straightforward: once signed writing and SignWriting are treated as real writing on their own terms, is this framework broad enough?

A companion paper under this same entry asks the larger theory-facing version of that question.

## Executive judgment

Framework judgment:

| Field | Judgment |
| --- | --- |
| Layer-level judgment | Correct and useful as a strong general grapholinguistic framework. |
| Full-path judgment | Strong but typologically incomplete once signed writing is treated as a full comparative case with plane-based written units, authored spatial composition, and signed-text infrastructure. |
| Main layer risk | A general theory of writing remains too dependent on familiar spoken-language writing categories when signed writing is treated as a pressure test. |

Refinement:

> `Structurally Strong but Typologically Incomplete`

This book does several things unusually well. It rejects crude speech-dependence, gives writing internal structure, takes visual form and materiality seriously, and handles typology with more self-awareness than much of the older literature.

Those are substantial strengths, and they are exactly what make the book worth pressing.

The deepest limitation is not that the book ignores writing structure. It is that the framework still appears not to provide a stable typological place for writing systems whose primary written structure is articulatory-spatial, plane-based, and kinetic.

SignWriting is exactly the kind of case that reveals whether the field's categories are merely broad enough for familiar systems, or broad enough for writing itself.

## What the book is trying to do

*Writing Systems and Their Use* presents itself as a broad grapholinguistic account of writing rather than as a narrow descriptive handbook. It aims to give writing serious comparative treatment across:

- language, speech, and writing
- graphetics and materiality
- graphematics and graphematic units
- orthography
- writing-system typology
- universals and wider comparative questions

That ambition matters. This is an attempt to define what counts as a serious modern theory of writing.

## Where the book is strongest

### 1. It moves beyond the weakest speech-dependence models

One of the book's clearest strengths is that it does not simply repeat the old claim that writing is nothing but visible speech. Its discussion of language, speech, and writing as modalities is more careful than much of the older literature. It distinguishes language from speech and writing, treats the autonomy debate seriously, and grants writing structures and functions that are not reducible to speech.

That is a real advance. The review should not reduce the book to the weakest caricatures of writing theory.

### 2. It gives writing internal structure

The treatment of graphematics is another genuine strength. The book does not leave writing as a merely visual shell placed over linguistic substance. It treats writing as a structured subsystem with its own units and relations. Graphematics, graphotactics, allography, larger graphematic units, and typology all receive real analytical weight.

It lets the book say more than:

- writing corresponds to language

It can also say:

- writing has internal organization that deserves description in its own right

### 3. It takes materiality and spatial organization seriously

The sections on descriptive graphetics and materialisation are especially useful. Many theories of writing become thin precisely where writing becomes visual, spatial, material, and socially embodied. This book does better there.

It treats as analytically meaningful:

- graphs and basic shapes
- spatial organization of the writing surface
- handwriting and typography
- readability, legibility, and processing
- the social meaning of visual variation

That does not solve the SignWriting problem by itself. It does mean the book is not blind to the visual and spatial dimensions of writing.

### 4. It handles typology with real self-awareness

The typology chapter is stronger than many comparable accounts because it openly acknowledges its own difficulties. The authors note that writing-system typologies are often:

- too broad
- based on only one criterion
- insufficiently fine-grained
- prone to conflating primary and secondary representational levels

Their distinction between primary and secondary phonography and morphography is a real conceptual contribution. It gives the field a more careful vocabulary than one finds in simpler typologies.

This matters because the book is not naively overconfident about its own classificatory tools.

## Where the book is most useful for SignWriting discussion

Even where the framework does not yet go far enough, it provides concepts that are genuinely helpful.

### 1. The autonomy discussion opens better ground than older models

The treatment of dependence versus autonomy does not simply dissolve writing into speech. That matters for signed language writing because it reopens the question of what writing can be when the language being written is not spoken.

The book does not fully answer that question, but it does make it possible to ask it more cleanly.

### 2. Graphetics and materiality make room for plane-based writing

The sections on spatial organization, material embodiment, handwriting, and typography help prevent the theory of writing from becoming purely correspondence-based. They keep visible features of writing that often disappear in more phonology-centered accounts.

That is one of the strongest points of contact for thinking about SignWriting as authored visual text rather than as a translational aid or notation overlay.

### 3. The typology is good enough to make its own limit visible

Weak typologies fail so crudely that they reveal little beyond their own narrowness. This book is better than that. Its typology is careful enough that the remaining problem becomes sharper:

it can describe many distinctions inside familiar writing traditions,

but it still may not have a primary category for a writing system whose authored structure is spatial and kinetic rather than primarily phonographic or morphographic.

## Where the framework begins to strain

The book is strongest where it expands the field. It is weakest where that expansion still does not seem expansive enough.

### 1. The typology still privileges familiar linguistic units

For all its sophistication, the main typological field of the book is still organized around relations between written units and linguistic units already familiar from speech-shaped traditions:

- phonemes
- syllables and moras
- morphemes

That yields the major contrast between phonography and morphography, alongside internal differentiation among segmentaries, syllabaries, abjads, abugidas, and related types.

This is a better and more self-aware map than many earlier ones. It may still not be broad enough.

The missing possibility is a writing type whose primary structural work is neither:

- the durable encoding of sound
- nor the primary writing of morphemes

but rather:

- the durable encoding of articulatory-spatial form

That possibility does not seem to have a stable place in the present framework.

### 2. Spatiality is acknowledged, but still not typologically primary

The book clearly knows that writing is spatial.

But spatiality still seems to function mostly as:

- organization
- arrangement
- surface structure
- material presentation

rather than as a primary representational type.

This distinction is decisive. For SignWriting, spatial composition is not merely how the writing is laid out after the fact. The plane is part of what is authored. The written sign is not simply a linearly recoverable sequence that later receives spatial presentation. The spatial arrangement is part of the written form itself.

If that is true, then a theory that can speak about spatiality only as a surrounding property will eventually misdescribe the writing system it is trying to include.

### 3. SignWriting is mentioned, but the field is not reorganized around the challenge it poses

The presence of SignWriting in the book matters. It shows that signed language writing is visible within the field. But visibility is not the same as conceptual consequence.

SignWriting does not appear to force the framework to revise its deepest typological assumptions. Instead, SignWriting appears more as something to be placed somewhere within the existing map than as something that might reveal the map itself to be incomplete.

## Chapter 2: modality and autonomy

The opening chapters are among the most helpful parts of the book.

The authors distinguish language from the modalities in which it is realized and reject the simplest claim that writing is merely visible speech. Their position is better described as partial autonomy: writing depends on language, but it also has structures and functions not reducible to speech.

That is a serious improvement over older accounts. It still leaves an unresolved question for signed language writing.

The unresolved question is whether writing is being theorized as dependent on language in a sufficiently modality-neutral way, or whether the structure of the discussion still quietly assumes speech-shaped linguistic units as the default center of writing. The book moves away from overt speech reductionism, but the later typology and grapheme discussions suggest that the field's deeper defaults may remain harder to escape.

So Chapter 2 should be preserved as a strength. It should also be read as the place where the review's central pressure begins.

## Chapter 4: grapheme and graphematic units

Chapter 4 is probably the strongest single location in the book for comparative analytical seriousness.

The grapheme discussion is broad, careful, and explicitly cross-linguistic. The authors do not simply assume that graphemes must correspond to phonemes in an alphabetic sense. They work through competing grapheme conceptions and build criteria meant to travel across different writing systems.

That is admirable work.

The pressure arises because even a broadened grapheme concept can still remain oriented toward relatively small units whose status is secured through correspondence to other linguistic units. For many writing systems, that is exactly the right place to begin. For SignWriting, the central writing problem may not be captured fully at that level.

The question is not only:

- what is the smallest distinctive written unit?

It is also:

- what is the primary written unit that makes the text model possible?

For SignWriting, the answer may be the written sign rather than the grapheme-like subunit. If so, the book's graphematic model remains powerful but not sufficient.

## Larger graphematic units and the problem of the written sign

The discussion of larger graphematic units adds another pressure point. The book's treatment of graphematic syllables and graphematic words is subtle and often useful, particularly in the way it distinguishes writing-internal structure from mere reflection of phonological units. It also handles cross-script differences more carefully than many simpler accounts.

Still, the overall unit hierarchy remains shaped by the kinds of structures that are already legible within established writing-system theory.

For SignWriting, the likely problem is not that the hierarchy has no larger units.

It is that the relevant larger unit may not be well described by imported expectations about wordhood, syllabic layering, or line-based segmentation.

If the written sign is the primary durable unit of signed text, then the theory may need not merely a more flexible grapheme but a different center of gravity.

## Reading processes: useful evidence, limited transfer

The psycholinguistic discussion is stronger than many comparable theoretical books because it explicitly asks whether grapholinguistic concepts have psychological correlates.

That is the right question. It matters that the book tries to connect:

- graph
- basic shape
- grapheme
- allography
- graphotactics

to actual processing.

At the same time, the reading literature it mobilizes remains heavily shaped by:

- word recognition
- alphabetic assumptions
- phonology-heavy traditions
- English-centered model building

The book explicitly acknowledges this alphabetism, which is to its credit. But acknowledgment does not fully solve the transfer problem.

A framework whose processing discussion remains deeply tied to alphabetic word recognition may have difficulty accommodating signed-language literacy, especially if signed writing depends on different perceptual and unit-level dynamics than the literature it cites was built to describe.

## Kinematography as a pressure test

This is where a candidate term such as `kinematography` becomes useful.

I do not mean its historical overlap with cinematography, and the term is not presented here as settled field vocabulary.

I mean it as a candidate typological term for writing systems whose primary structural work is the durable encoding of articulatory-spatial-kinetic form.

Placed beside:

- phonography
- morphography

it names a third possibility.

Used carefully, `kinematography` would denote a writing type in which written units primarily preserve the kinematic structure of signing:

- handshape
- location
- movement
- orientation
- spatial relations

This is not offered here as a settled final label or as a complete typological proposal. It is offered as a pressure test.

If a term like this becomes necessary in order to describe what SignWriting is doing structurally, then the current typology is not merely awkward at one edge. It is missing a class of writing-system organization.

That would be a major conclusion. It would mean the limitation is not just classificatory inconvenience. It is typological incompleteness.

## What the book actually establishes

The strongest claims the book supports are:

- writing is not reducible to speech
- writing has internal structure deserving independent analysis
- visual and material form matter to the study of writing
- typology must distinguish primary and secondary representational levels
- comparative grapholinguistics is possible and worth pursuing

Those claims should be preserved. What the book does not yet appear to establish is:

- that its present typology is sufficient for all writing systems
- that signed language writing can be housed cleanly inside the phonography/morphography field as currently drawn
- that spatiality has already been granted the correct status when it becomes part of the primary authored form

## Final assessment

This book should not be reviewed as though it were just one more simplistic account that writing equals speech written down. That would be analytically weak.

It should be reviewed as one of the strongest current attempts to theorize writing comparatively, and precisely for that reason as a framework worth pressing at its limits.

Its deepest contribution is that it gives writing real internal structure.

Its deepest limitation is that it still appears not to grant signed language writing a fully adequate typological position.

That is why the best judgment is not simple rejection.

More specifically:

> a strong grapholinguistic framework that remains typologically incomplete once signed language writing is treated as a real writing type rather than as a marginal special case

That is what makes the book both valuable and reviewable.
