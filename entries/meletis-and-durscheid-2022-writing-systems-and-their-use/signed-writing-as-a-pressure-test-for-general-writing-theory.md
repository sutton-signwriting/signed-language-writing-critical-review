# Signed Writing as a Pressure Test for General Writing Theory

**A companion paper to the review of Meletis and Dürscheid (2022)**

The review of *Writing Systems and Their Use* asks a source-centered question:

- what does this book argue, and how adequate is it once signed writing is treated seriously?

This companion asks the broader theory-facing version:

- what does signed writing reveal about the present limits of general writing theory when one of its strongest recent grapholinguistic statements is used as the lead case?

The claim of this paper is not that Meletis and Dürscheid fail in any simple or dismissive sense. On the contrary, they are useful precisely because the book is strong. It gives writing internal structure, treats the autonomy question more carefully than older work, takes materiality seriously, and handles typology with unusual self-awareness.

That is what makes it the right lead case.

Weak theories do not pressure the field very much. They fail too crudely. A strong theory reveals more. It shows where the field has genuinely advanced and where, even after that advance, some assumptions remain too speech-shaped, too correspondence-centered, or too narrow to house signed writing cleanly.

So the purpose of this paper is not to repeat the review. It is to argue that signed writing is not merely another example to be accommodated by grapholinguistics. It is a pressure test for what grapholinguistics can presently describe at all.

## Why Meletis and Dürscheid is the right lead case

There are at least three reasons this book is the right anchor for a broader theory-facing companion.

### 1. It is unusually strong on writing as writing

The book does not reduce writing to visible speech in the weakest older sense. It treats writing as a structured subsystem with its own units, relations, and material forms. That makes it better than older frameworks that fail before signed writing even enters the room.

### 2. It is broad enough to matter beyond one script family

This is not a narrow handbook of one orthography or one historical tradition. It is an attempt to say what modern grapholinguistics should look like. If a book like this still strains once signed writing is treated seriously, then the problem is not local to one author. It becomes a field-level issue.

### 3. Its limits are therefore informative

If a weak theory cannot house signed writing, little follows. If a strong one still cannot house it cleanly, then signed writing is telling us something deeper about the categories themselves.

That is the pressure test.

## Signed writing is not a marginal case

One persistent habit in the study of writing is to treat signed writing as though it were an unusual edge case. On that view, one starts with the "real" writing systems of the world, builds categories from them, and only later asks where signed writing might fit.

That approach gets the order wrong.

Signed writing is not merely difficult because it is signed. It is theoretically important because it forces explicit answers to questions that many writing theories can leave partially implicit when dealing only with familiar spoken-language traditions:

- what is the primary written unit?
- what counts as representational mapping?
- when is spatial organization part of authorship rather than later display?
- how much of writing theory is actually about writing, and how much is inherited from speech-shaped cases?

These are not niche questions. They are central questions.

That is why signed writing should be treated not as a marginal example but as a theory-shaping one.

## Pressure point 1: autonomy is stronger than many theories can comfortably admit

Modern grapholinguistics has already moved beyond the crudest speech-dependence models. Meletis and Dürscheid help make that move explicit by treating writing as a structured object of study rather than a mere derivative shell.

That is real progress.

But signed writing presses the autonomy question further.

It is one thing to say that writing is not exhausted by speech.

It is another to describe a writing system whose primary written organization is not comfortably derivable from the segmental assumptions of spoken-language traditions at all.

This is where signed writing increases the pressure. It asks whether writing is being theorized as dependent on language in a genuinely modality-neutral way, or whether speech-shaped linguistic structure still quietly occupies the center even after explicit reductionism has been rejected.

If signed writing cannot be described without special strain, then autonomy has been granted only partway.

## Pressure point 2: the grapheme is not enough

One of the strongest parts of *Writing Systems and Their Use* is its treatment of the grapheme. The book does real comparative work there. It does not merely recycle alphabetic common sense. It tries to build a grapheme concept that can travel across writing systems.

That effort should be preserved.

But signed writing exposes a deeper problem:

- the grapheme may remain too small, too correspondence-centered, or too structurally low to serve as the main explanatory center

For many writing systems, asking for the smallest distinctive written unit is exactly the right first question.

For SignWriting, the central question may not be:

- what is the smallest distinctive written unit?

but rather:

- what is the primary durable written unit that makes signed text possible?

If the answer is the written sign, then grapholinguistics needs more than a broadened grapheme. It needs a different center of gravity.

That does not abolish graphemes.

It does mean that signed writing can reveal the limits of treating graphematic minimality as the main gateway into writing-system description.

## Pressure point 3: larger graphematic units remain too line-shaped

The same issue reappears one level up.

Grapholinguistic accounts of larger units often move toward:

- graphematic syllables
- graphematic words
- directional groupings
- line-based segmentation

These are subtle and useful concepts.

They are still often shaped by writing traditions where linear accumulation is the dominant logic of the text surface.

Signed writing asks whether there are writing systems for which the relevant larger unit is not well captured by imported assumptions about syllabic layering, wordhood, or line progression. If the written sign is the primary durable unit of signed text, then the larger-unit problem is not simply an extension of familiar line-based categories.

That again suggests not that grapholinguistics is useless, but that it may still be too comfortable with the kinds of units that are already legible in spoken-language scripts.

## Pressure point 4: spatiality is still too secondary

This is one of the clearest places where signed writing exposes the present limit of otherwise strong theory.

Modern grapholinguistics is much better than older accounts at recognizing that writing is visual, material, and spatial. Meletis and Dürscheid are good on this. They discuss:

- graphs and basic shapes
- layout
- handwriting and typography
- readability and legibility
- visual variation

But signed writing raises a more severe question:

- what if spatiality is not just organization around writing, but part of the written form itself?

For SignWriting, spatial arrangement is not merely where the writing appears after it has been encoded in some deeper linear order. The plane is part of what is written. The written sign is authored as a spatial composition.

Once that is true, spatiality can no longer remain mostly a graphetic or material side issue. It has to become typologically and structurally primary.

This is where many writing theories, even good ones, begin to strain. They can acknowledge space. They are less ready to center it.

## Pressure point 5: typology remains incomplete

This is the point at which the pressure becomes unmistakably typological.

If writing-system typology is still chiefly organized around relations between written units and familiar linguistic units such as:

- phonemes
- syllables
- morphemes

then signed writing will always enter the framework as either:

- a difficult special case
- an awkward mixed case
- or an under-described anomaly

That should be taken as a warning sign.

It suggests that the typology is not merely missing a label. It may be missing a class of writing-system organization.

This is why candidate terms such as `kinematography` become useful. The point of such a term is not to rename SignWriting for rhetorical effect or to introduce settled field vocabulary prematurely. It is to ask whether a broader typology needs a category for writing whose primary structural work is the durable encoding of articulatory-spatial-kinetic form.

Even if that particular label is not retained, the pressure it names is real.

If grapholinguistics cannot yet say clearly where such systems belong, then its typology remains incomplete.

## What kinematography would have to mean

At this stage, `kinematography` should remain a provisional typological probe rather than a settled category.

But the probe needs enough shape to be useful.

In its strongest form, `kinematography` would name a writing-system type in which the primary representational mapping preserves articulatory-spatial-kinetic structure as durable written form. Its written unit would be organized around composed bodily or manual action rather than primarily around sound segments, syllables, or morphemes.

That formulation matters because it prevents several misleading reductions.

It prevents `kinematography` from meaning only movement.

It prevents it from meaning only visual layout.

It prevents it from being reduced to iconicity.

And it prevents the term from becoming merely another name for SignWriting.

SignWriting is the clearest current pressure case, not necessarily the only possible case. The term names a typological possibility: writing whose primary structure durably encodes articulatory-spatial-kinetic form. Other systems would have to be evaluated by whether they meet that criterion, not by whether they resemble SignWriting externally.

A system would count as kinematographic writing only if several conditions hold:

- its primary written forms encode articulatory-spatial features
- spatial composition is part of authorship rather than merely layout
- the primary written unit is a composed action or sign unit, not only a linear sequence of segmental values
- it can produce durable written text independent of video
- it is not adequately classified as phonographic or morphographic without distortion

This last point needs care.

One could call SignWriting a kind of phonography if `phonography` is generalized to mean any writing of language form. SignWriting does represent formational features of signed language: handshape, movement, location, orientation, spatial relation, and other visible features of signing.

But that generalization risks hiding the distinction signed writing makes visible. The form being written is not sound-form. It is embodied, spatial, and kinetic form.

`Kinematography` is proposed not to deny formal representation, but to prevent sound-based terminology from silently governing the typology.

The term should also not automatically include every notation of movement. Movement notation, research transcription, and language writing may all preserve aspects of bodily action, but they do not necessarily occupy the same textual role. The question is whether a system functions as writing for language users, producing durable readable text, rather than only as analytic transcription or specialist notation.

For that reason, `kinematography` should be held carefully.

It is not yet a finished typology.

It is a name for the category that general writing theory may need if signed writing is taken seriously, and its adequacy remains an open question rather than a settled terminological contribution.

## Pressure point 6: reading theory may still be too alphabetically loaded

Another place where signed writing reveals stress is the theory of reading.

General writing theory often relies, directly or indirectly, on psycholinguistic work shaped by:

- word recognition
- alphabetic literacy
- phonology-heavy tasks
- English-centered experimental traditions

Meletis and Dürscheid are better than most in acknowledging this problem. But acknowledgment is not the same as resolution.

If signed writing depends on different units, different perceptual chunking, and different relations between spatial composition and recognition, then grapholinguistic reading theory cannot simply treat the signed case as an application of already adequate models. It has to ask whether the dominant evidence base was theory-forming only because the scripts under study were too narrow a sample.

Signed writing therefore pressures not only typology, but processing theory as well.

## What signed writing reveals about grapholinguistics

Taken together, these pressure points support a broader conclusion.

Signed writing reveals that even strong grapholinguistics may still be:

- too correspondence-centered
- too comfortable with small-unit analysis
- too willing to leave spatiality at the level of arrangement rather than authored structure
- too typologically narrow in its primary categories
- too dependent on reading models built from alphabetic traditions

This does not mean grapholinguistics is failing.

It means grapholinguistics is being asked to grow.

And that is the right way to frame the issue.

Signed writing should not be used merely to refute writing theory.

It should be used to improve it.

## Why this should remain a companion and not replace the review

The review of Meletis and Dürscheid does a different job and should remain disciplined:

- what the book says
- what it gets right
- where it strains

This companion moves at a different level. It uses the book as the lead case for a larger claim:

- signed writing is a pressure test for the adequacy of general writing theory

That is why the two pieces belong together but should not be collapsed.

The review preserves fairness to the source.

The companion makes the broader theoretical consequence explicit.

## Conclusion

If *Writing Systems and Their Use* were a weak book, signed writing would prove little by resisting its categories.

But it is not weak.

It is one of the stronger recent attempts to theorize writing comparatively.

That is exactly why it matters that signed writing still pressures it.

The lesson is not that grapholinguistics should retreat from comparison.

The lesson is that signed writing must be treated as a full comparative case, capable of forcing revisions in:

- autonomy theory
- grapheme theory
- larger-unit theory
- typology
- spatial modeling
- and reading theory

Signed writing is therefore not simply another object for grapholinguistics to classify.

It is one of the cases by which grapholinguistics should now be judged.
