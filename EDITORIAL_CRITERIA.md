# Signed Language Writing Review Series Editorial Criteria

This document sets the standing editorial standard for `Signed Language Writing Critical Review Series`.

Its purpose is not to explain the series.

Its purpose is to keep the series:

- rigorous
- trustworthy
- readable outside the SignWriting community
- critical without sounding prosecutorial
- structurally fair to alternatives
- and constructive rather than critique-only

## The Central Editorial Problem

The main risk is not weak analysis.

The main risk is that accurate analysis will be read as advocacy because the series is closely aligned with SignWriting and Formal SignWriting as the strongest currently documented path for durable signed-language writing infrastructure.

So the editorial task is not false neutrality.

It is disciplined legitimacy.

The series must show that it has:

- a real evaluative lens
- a visible basis for its comparisons
- bounded claims
- charitable treatment of alternatives
- and a constructive horizon beyond diagnosis

## Standing Position

The series may state plainly that:

- SignWriting is a real writing system
- Formal SignWriting is a real technical text model
- SignWriting currently provides the most complete documented path from signed writing to durable text infrastructure

The series should not imply that:

- SignWriting is the only imaginable path
- every alternative is worthless
- the field's open questions are already settled
- current infrastructural completeness automatically proves universal superiority in every domain

The strongest defensible comparative claim is narrower:

> SignWriting currently appears to offer the broadest documented end-to-end path across writing practice, structured text, tooling, and infrastructure, while other approaches tend to solve narrower problems or operate at different layers.

## Editorial Criteria

### 1. The series must be explicit about its lens

The series is not neutral arbitration from nowhere.

It is a coherent evaluative program built around:

- layer-level and full-path distinction
- structural adequacy
- written-form analysis
- text-model adequacy
- evidence, authority, and data-object discipline
- and long-term infrastructure viability

That lens should be made visible early, not smuggled in as though it were self-evident.

### 2. Comparative claims must separate layer-level success from full-path adequacy

Do not say an approach is incomplete in the abstract.

Say where it is incomplete:

- as public writing
- as notation
- as a text model
- as a semantic representation
- as infrastructure
- as a handwriting practice
- as a pedagogical bridge

This is the main safeguard against unfairness.

Every review should state, where relevant:

- what the source accomplishes at its own layer
- whether that accomplishment supports a full signed-text infrastructure path
- what remains unresolved for a stronger full-path claim

### 3. The baseline must remain strong, but not absolute

The series may treat SignWriting plus Formal SignWriting as the current strongest baseline for durable signed text.

It must also state where that baseline remains open, limited, or under-evidenced.

At minimum, the series should remain candid about:

- large-scale literacy evidence
- adoption unevenness across regions
- handwriting fluency and everyday uptake
- interoperability with mainstream software and standards
- long-term empirical comparison with other practical pathways

### 4. Alternatives must be described by function before judgment

Before critique, identify what an alternative actually does well.

Examples:

- transcriptional precision
- pedagogical simplicity
- handwriting experimentation
- semantic formalization
- community expressiveness
- research notation

Do not begin by measuring every system against the full infrastructure stack if the system is not claiming to solve that whole stack.

Do measure it against that stack if it is being proposed as a full writing path.

### 5. The series must distinguish niche value from full-path adequacy

This distinction should be explicit and recurring.

A system may be:

- valuable in a niche
- useful in education
- strong for transcription
- interesting for handwriting
- effective for semantic software

without thereby constituting a full path to durable public text infrastructure.

This lets the series be generous without becoming vague.

### 6. Critique must be paired with constructive consequence

No entry should end with diagnosis alone if a constructive implication can be stated.

At minimum, reviews should identify one or more of:

- what the source still contributes
- what research is now needed
- what interoperability question remains open
- what data would clarify the dispute
- what authority or data-governance boundary must be named
- what infrastructure task follows

The series should increasingly read as:

- critique in the service of construction

not:

- critique as an end in itself

### 7. Trust must be earned through boundedness

Strong claims are acceptable.

Unbounded claims are not.

Prefer:

- `currently the most complete documented path`
- `best read as`
- `strong at this layer`
- `does not yet establish`
- `solves a narrower problem`
- `remains incomplete on`

Avoid:

- `the only real solution`
- `everything else fails`
- `proves decisively`
- `settles the question`

### 8. The series must remain readable to non-insiders

Do not assume familiarity with:

- FSW
- SWU
- ISWA
- signbox structure
- the series' critique vocabulary

Brief orientation should appear at first need.

The prose should not require prior loyalty to the platform.

Naming should be specific without becoming heavy. Use `Sutton SignWriting` when the formal identity of the writing system needs to be explicit, then use `SignWriting` naturally when no ambiguity is likely. Do not explain the shortening in reader-facing prose.

### 9. The framework must remain a tool, not the protagonist

The framework should sharpen judgment.

It should not overpower the source.

Readers should leave feeling:

- the source was read closely
- the judgment was earned
- the method helped

not:

- the source was only a pretext for repeating house categories

### 10. The series must sound open to external evidence

The series should welcome:

- independent literacy studies
- comparative corpus work
- adoption data
- handwriting studies
- interoperability experiments
- standards-facing technical review

It should not sound as though others merely need to recognize what is already obvious.

## Entry-Level Publication Test

An entry is publication-ready only if it satisfies all of the following:

- it states the source's aim before criticizing it
- it identifies what the source gets right before escalation
- it says what layer or layers are being judged
- it distinguishes layer-level success from full-path adequacy where relevant
- it names evidence, authority, and data-object limits where they affect the claim
- it avoids implying that SignWriting wins by default
- it states at least one constructive consequence or open question
- it keeps the strongest claim no broader than the evidence allows
- it sounds like a review, not a defense brief

## Series-Level Publication Test

The series is publication-ready only if:

- the front matter makes the lens explicit without pretending neutrality
- the front matter also makes the series sound open rather than closed
- the internal baseline pieces are strong without reading as self-congratulation
- external reviews preserve genuine contributions of rival work
- the series includes a visible constructive horizon, not only diagnosis
- alternative systems are acknowledged for their real niches
- SignWriting's own open problems remain visible

## Standing Editorial Checks

The series should maintain these checks across new and revised entries.

### 1. Keep the series lens visible

- State plainly that the series offers a coherent evaluative lens, not neutral arbitration.
- State plainly that the lens is oriented toward durable writing, text structure, and infrastructure adequacy.
- Keep the distinction between layer-level success and full-path adequacy visible.

### 2. Preserve the constructive horizon

- Keep reviews connected to open research, tooling, standards, corpora, education, data governance, and interoperability.
- Use critique as a filter for building, not only judging.

### 3. Preserve visible pluralism

- Name the real niches of adjacent-purpose systems.
- Distinguish transcription value, pedagogical value, handwriting value, semantic value, dataset value, rendering value, and full-path infrastructure value.
- Make sure alternatives are not introduced only in order to be narrowed.

### 4. Keep self-critique visible

- Keep SignWriting's own open problems visible.
- Keep adoption, readability, handwriting, interoperability, institutional support, and standards questions visible.
- Make sure the internal baseline does not read as closure.

### 5. Invite external evidence

- Name the kinds of evidence that would most improve the field.
- Treat unresolved questions as research opportunities, not embarrassments.
- Welcome independent empirical work, standards-facing review, corpus work, and tool interoperability testing.

## Operating Rule

If a future edit makes the series sound more certain but less trustworthy, it is a bad edit.

If a future edit makes the series more candid, more comparative, and more constructive without weakening the real analysis, it is a good edit.
